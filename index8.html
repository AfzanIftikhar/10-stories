Things fall apart

"Things Fall Apart" is a novel written by Nigerian author Chinua Achebe, first published in 1958. It tells the story
of Okonkwo, an Igbo man who struggles to adapt to the changes brought by British colonialism and Christian
missionaries in his village of Umuofia.

Summary of the story

Part1: Okonkwo's Rise

The novel begins with Okonkwo, a respected warrior and wrestler in Umuofia, known for his strength and
determination. He is haunted by the memory of his father, Unoka, who was lazy and unsuccessful. Okonkwo works tirelessly to build his wealth and reputation, eventually gaining multiple titles in the clan.

Part 2: Arrival of the Europeans

The arrival of white missionaries and colonial government officials disrupts the traditional way of life in Umuofia. The missionaries introduce Christianity and gradually gain converts, including Okonkwo's own son, Nwoye, who rejects his father's beliefs.

Part 3: Conflict and Tragedy

Tensions rise between the traditional Igbo culture and the new influences brought by the Europeans. Okonkwo, resistant to change and desperate to maintain his clan's way of life, takes drastic actions to resist the colonizers. This leads to a tragic event where Okonkwo kills a British messenger, and the village leaders, fearing reprisals, do not support him

Part 4: Fall of Okonkwo

Okonkwo, realizing that his clan will not go to war against the British, hangs himself in despair. His suicide is a symbolic act of defiance against the colonizers but also reflects his inability to adapt to the changing world. The novel ends with the district commissioner arriving to write a book about the Igbo people's customs, trivializing their culture and history.

THEME:

Colonialism and Cultural Clash: Achebe explores the destructive impact of colonialism on African societies, portraying how European intrusion disrupts and undermines traditional African values and structures. Tradition vs. Change: The novel vividly depicts the conflict between the traditional customs and beliefs of the Igbo people and the modernizing forces of European colonization and Christianity. Hubris and Tragic Flaw: Okonkwo's tragic downfall is driven by his excessive pride (hubris) and his stubborn refusal to adapt to changing circumstances, ultimately leading to his alienation from his community and his tragic end.
